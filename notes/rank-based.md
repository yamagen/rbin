# Rank-based binning notes

## Purpose

`rbin` is a small command-line tool for observing the shape of numerical distributions.

It originated from the older `sturges` program, which was used to summarize score distributions for examination reports and thesis data appendices. The purpose was not to perform full statistical analysis, but to produce a compact summary that could be pasted into a limited space:

- number of observations
- minimum, mean, maximum
- standard deviation and standard error
- skewness and kurtosis
- simple rank/bin frequency table

This compact output made it possible to inspect the character of a dataset without consuming much space.

## Why not just use R or MATLAB?

R and MATLAB can produce histograms easily. However, `rbin` is intended as a small component in a text-processing pipeline.

For example:

```sh
awk '$1 > 0 {print $13}' data.txt | rbin -c -T0
```

This makes the binning step visible, repeatable, and easy to connect with other tools such as `cw`, `graph`, Graphviz, and shell scripts.

The aim is not to replace statistical software, but to keep the observation process close to the data.

## Binning rules

`rbin` currently supports three binning rules:

- `-T0`: Sturges rule
- `-T1`: Scott rule
- `-T2`: Freedman-Diaconis rule

The default is Sturges.

Sturges is useful for obtaining a rough overview of the distribution. Scott uses the standard deviation and may become too coarse when the distribution has a long tail. Freedman-Diaconis uses the interquartile range and is useful for checking finer details of the central part and tail behavior.

In the present use case, Sturges is often suitable for figures in papers because it gives a readable overview. Freedman-Diaconis is useful as a supplementary check. Scott may be too coarse for some cw/z-value distributions.

## Relation to cw

In the cw workflow, cooccurrence weight is treated as a kind of weight. Like body weight, extremely light and extremely heavy cases are relatively rare, while many cases gather around the middle.

The important point for visualization is not the center of the distribution but the tails.

- Left tail: weak, light, often generic or function-like cooccurrence
- Center: ordinary, frequent, less distinctive cooccurrence
- Right tail: strong, heavy, content-word-like or topic-forming cooccurrence

The main target of WakaGraph/wg visualization is the right tail.

## z-values and the right tail

The z-value is used to show the relative position of each cooccurrence weight:

```text
z = (weight - mean) / standard deviation
```

This does not transform the distribution into a normal distribution. It only expresses how far each value is from the mean.

Before using z ≒ 1 as a practical threshold, the distribution shape must first be checked. If the distribution is approximately unimodal and bell-shaped, then z ≒ 1 can be used as a rough guide to the transition into the right tail.

The threshold is therefore not imposed in advance. It is used after observing the actual distribution.

## Principle

The visualization process should remain explainable from the ground up:

1. divide waka into units
2. make word pairs
3. count the pairs
4. calculate cooccurrence weight
5. inspect the distribution of weights
6. identify the right tail
7. visualize the strong cooccurrence network

This keeps the visualization connected to the text. The graph is not a decorative output generated by a black box. It is a way of returning from counted relations to the reading of waka.
